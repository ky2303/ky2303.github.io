## Seeing

Like basically everyone in the field, I have been experimenting with LLMs for both professional work and side projects. I have read and heard numerous takes from people of all skill levels declaring these new tools either useless autocomplete that will lead to the ensloppified downfall of society or the ultimate singularity-bringing technology that will take all our jobs and bring in the next golden age. Personally, I have had a middle-of-the-road mindset of "I will believe it when I see it". Either way, I always enjoy learning new tools and LLMs have been no exception. Since the initial launch of ChatGPT in 2022, I have been testing its capabilities. When Ollama was released, I was excited about the potential of writing custom modelfiles and deploying models locally just like Docker containers.

Throughout my learning, I have definitely seen flashes of new possibilities in computing but I have been skeptical of the real value. For most of the craze, I feel that the suggested ceiling of AI tech is not as high as proponents suggest and have been leaning more towards "I don't believe it since I don't see it". In my day job as a Digital Forensics and Incident Response analyst, we are implementing lots of new AI tools, yet I have not seen anything truly made a big difference in speeding up overall response times or containing and remediating true positives. A lot of what has been sold as "game-changing" addons in our vendor products honestly feel like SOAR automations with an attached chatbot. I am all for efficiency and shortcuts but in DFIR, trying to [outsource understanding](https://youtu.be/96jN2OCOfLs?si=jsqQYfwGlPwXrGBf&t=1685) just feels like extra work. If you are writing an incident report, everything needs to be verified so if you are using summaries from an agent you will end up looking at the logs and artifacts anyways (the fun part of the job).

However, sometime earlier this year, with the rise of agents, tools, and skills, I have started "see it" and am getting close to "believing it". I have not participated in CTFs in a while, but I lead an annual internal CTF for Cybersecurity Awareness Month and thus, I have been following the discourse on how disruptive LLMs have been for the competitive scene. Projects like [BoxPwner](https://github.com/0ca/BoxPwnr) really show the progress new models have been making if you look through the [traces](https://boxpwnr.info/platform.html?platform=htb-challenges). (HackTheBox traces are not shown for site policy reasons but you can still see the improvement over time and view traces for other CTF platforms.) When Claude Mythos and [Project Glasswing](https://www.anthropic.com/project/glasswing) were announced, the security world received an abrupt, unavoidable wake up call. High-severity vulnerabilities in deep niches like Linux privilege escalation seemingly popped up multiple times a week. I have worked on multiple incidents with weird, verbosely-commented malware where I used to mostly see obfuscation. Seeing things like [EvilTokens](https://www.huntress.com/blog/device-code-phishing-ai-mfa-bypass) and the supply chain compromises by [TeamPCP](https://www.wired.com/story/teampcp-software-supply-chain-attack-spree-github/) and [Shai Hulud](https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/alerts/2025/09/23/widespread-supply-chain-compromise-impacting-npm-ecosystem) in the wild felt like watching [the battering ram breach the gates of Minas Tirith](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=k9-Jx-DjDaU). Clearly, technically advanced threat actors have embraced AI tools, forcing defenders to scramble on writing detections and patching faster than ever. In retrospect, I think security was bound to be the first mover in discovering high-value, real world use cases due to the natural curiosity, technical talent, and hacker mindset of the security community. I am now starting to believe that it is only a matter of time before power users in other industries come along and do the same for their industry-standard software. Additionally, things generally appear to be accelerating with the parallel progress of larger models, more compute, and the scaffolding like harnesses and skills being built around models. 


## Experiments

**Summarizing Threat Intelligence**

One aspect of my job that has always bothered me is the difficulty of disseminating the latest threat intelligence surrounding social engineering. I could not understand why it took so much effort to get relevant information in front of users and then once that was accomplished, why so many people still fell for simple phishing lures. I read a few books on habits and marketing and concluded that the problems boiled down to apathy. Employees don't want to read a whole essay about not clicking links or password habits when they open their email in the morning. They just want to read things that are relevant to getting their projects and tasks for the day completed so they can go home to enjoy time with their children. I think there are great companies in the Awareness and Education space, like [Pistachio](https://pistachioapp.com/blog/security-awareness-has-a-control-problem) and [Hoxhunt](https://hoxhunt.com/), that have also come to this conclusion and are thinking of different ways to do security awareness. However, recent research has shown that short, frequent, and timely nudges are a much better way to keep social engineering front of mind for many employees, rather than the surprise-attack phishing simulations and annual computer-based training packaged with the compliance videos. In the end, awareness is still a regulatory and insurance requirement that a lot of CISOs have to check off.

At the time, I was mainly tinkering with Ollama and Cursor. Through a series of loops, Cursor with Sonnet was able to spit out a website that receives a link to any security blog or article, summarizes the contents with a local Deepseek model, categorizes threats based on industry and department, and writes a simple quiz, giving a prize to employees who got everything correct. The idea for this site was to allow security teams to quickly spin up awareness campaigns and reward users for knowledge retention. This was my first attempt at vibe coding something other than simple helper scripts. What surprised me was the quality of summaries by the local Deepseek model, correctly categorizing threats by target industries and departments, and generating recommendations for users to defend themselves. However, it was frustrating to get the model to output json and I ended up having Sonnet write shim code to fix this. I may revisit this project in the future but for now I have other obligations.

**Bug Hunting**

While I have been extremely lucky that my workplace has provided basically every new model for use at on the job, I have not seen much use for a paid personal plan to any model provider. I believe in my capabilites for side projects and I want to maintain coding and hacking skills since Offsec has a strict no AI policy for their exams. I would hate to get to a point in an exam and wish I could use an agent or chatbot for some help. Nonetheless, I have always loved homelabbing and repurposing old hardware so I could not help but see how far I could push an old 2018 Mac Mini I had lying around gathering dust. (Specs: Intel i7-8700B, 32GB mem, Intel iGPU.) After installing Ubuntu, llama-cpp, and Claude Code cli, I downloaded a bunch of models from Huggingface and tested their capabilities. Starting with simple "Hello world" programs and scripts, I ended up with a decent prompt to write a program that finds the prime factors of an input number. Lots of models that were able to run on the old hardware simply failed at this task, but Qwen2.5 was the first one to complete it. (However, it took almost 12 hours to finish.) The big breakthrough was Google's release of the Gemma4 models. It was able to finish the prime factors task in a reasonable time and the code was much more efficient. This was the first time I felt confident moving up the difficulty ladder and I decided to test its capability with new ideas. Around this time, the Mythos hype was in full swing and I found some crazy explanations on how Nicholas Carlini, a security researcher at Anthropic and formerly at DeepMind, was searching for vulnerabilities in code. I couldn't believe how simple his [method](https://securitycryptographywhatever.com/2026/03/25/ai-bug-finding/) sounded. He basically scripted out an iterative process to ask Claude to find vulnerabilities in each file in a codebase and then verify it with a second prompt. Obviously this method would rack up token credits so I decided to see if Gemma could do this locally without access to Carlini's [unlimited slot machine pulls](https://sockpuppet.org/blog/2026/03/30/vulnerability-research-is-cooked/). 


![vibecoding is gambling]({{ "../assets/2026-06-12-Local-LLM-experiments/vibecoding-gambling.png" | relative_url }}) 

I wrote my own bash scripts based on Carlini's description and pulled down some repos of open source tools that I use often. Unfortunately, I quickly ran into issues as the context window would become too massive for my poor little Mac Mini. Llama-cpp would continuously crash and Claude would return API errors. I chalked this up as a failed experiment and will maybe revisit it with a [more refined approach](https://claude.com/blog/using-llms-to-secure-source-code), when I get new hardware, small models become even better, or when I finally bite the bullet and get a paid subscription to a frontier model. That being said, I disagree with those running this type of setup and flooding Github issues for open source projects with slop so maybe the it's best that this type of workflow is cost-prohibitive and good old-fashioned, organic, farm-to-table code review is left to the experts.


**Documenting Social Engineering**

Since I started studying for the OSCP exam, I have been a huge fan of the [GTFOBins](https://gtfobins.org/) project, which lists and categorizes unix binaries that can be abused for privilege escalation. The subsequent copycat projects of [LOLBAS](https://lolbas-project.github.io/) and [LOLRMM](https://lolrmm.io/) have also been extremely helpful at my dayjob in IR work. Community projects like these are essential for the defense community for crowd-sourcing knowledge, detecting malicious behavior, and hardening our environments. The fact that they are open-source also made it easy to go from idea to working prototype when the idea hit me to create another copycat for social engineering techniques. Using the hardware setup from my bug hunting experiment, I pulled down the repo for LOLBAS and gave Claude a simple prompt with a `/goal` to implement. I was impressed with how far Claude got but realized after a few loops that it wasn't quite getting the Github Pages code all the way to a working state. Additionally, it hallucinated some details of the techniques I gave it and I don't have any web search skills so its ability to research was limited. Although I had to hand write some code and fill in the blanks for content, I was still pretty impressed with how far lil ol' Mac Mini was able to get on this task. I was able to publish a working (though maybe a bit sloppy) [site](https://lolsocialengineering.github.io/) in a weekend. Although, I believe I could have completed this over the same time with some bash scripts and elbow grease a few years ago, it is amazing that I can now have a local agent complete it over a weekend where I had prior commitments and childcare duties. This project is still a work in progress and if anyone on the dead internet sees this and would like to contribute, please do.

## Conclusion

Messing around with local LLMs has been fun and offers the ability to complete many more fun little side quests from my main IT work and offsec studies. I think we are seeing an acceleration of capabilities on both the high and low end and it is exciting to think about where we can possibly go from here. I plan on continuing to tinker with these current projects and also have other ideas for the future. Using LLMs for reverse engineering and malware analysis is one area of security research that has been getting lots of buzz but I have not really had the chance to play around with yet. I would love to work on something like this soon.

That being said, I understand the general public pushback against AI and LLMs and the resistance to being force-fed chatbots in everyone's productivity software. While I am neither an AI expert nor a financial expert, I question the sustainability of the contracts being inked for model access. Even if the subsidized token allowances dry up, enterprises who purchase security products know that vendors will always try to crank up prices overtime and I worry that mass job cuts to free up AI budget may prove to be shortsighted. We are already seeing big companies like [Uber](https://www.forbes.com/sites/janakirammsv/2026/05/17/uber-burns-its-2026-ai-budget-in-four-months-on-claude-code/) and [Microsoft](https://finance.yahoo.com/sectors/technology/articles/ai-cost-crisis-emerges-claude-195612806.html) cut back on token allowances for devs and their flagship products feel the same, if not worse, than when the craze started. I suppose if any silver lining can be found from a cooldown in this frenzy, it's that maybe hardware prices will stop skyrocketing and I can start working on my homelab again. 🥰
